Make Histories, not War

After serving in World War I, J.R.R. Tolkien shifted his focus to the myths, histories, and languages he had been crafting, eventually naming this body of work the Legendarium. In 1924, he became a professor at Leeds University, where he furthered his studies in philology and medieval literature.

In 1936, Tolkien gave his renowned lecture, “Beowulf: The Monsters and the Critics”, which significantly influenced modern understanding of the epic poem. Around the same time, he began writing The Hobbit, famously beginning the story with the sentence, “In a hole in the ground there lived a hobbit.”

By 1945, Tolkien was a professor at Merton College, Oxford, where he taught English Language and Literature until his retirement in 1959. During his time at Oxford, he became part of a literary group known as The Inklings, which included notable figures like C.S. Lewis, the author of The Chronicles of Narnia. Together, they shared and critiqued each other’s work, fostering a creative environment that shaped some of the greatest literary works of the 20th century.

Tolkien's contributions to literature and scholarship earned him the honor of being appointed a Commander of the Order of the British Empire (CBE).
